History & Origins

American football has roots in post-Civil War culture, where young men needed a new arena to prove strength, discipline, and toughness. According to the Radiolab episode "American Football," early games at Harvard and Yale were brutal, chaotic, and often dangerous, yet celebrated as symbols of masculinity.

The first Harvard–Yale football game at Hamilton Park on 13 November 1875. Photograph by Oscar White/Corbis, via The Guardian.

Early Ivy League Football

Carlisle Indian School

Native American players used football as a tool to challenge racist stereotypes. Despite a system built to erase Native culture, Carlisle players earned national respect, redefining what Americans believed about identity and equality.

Carlisle Indian School football team in 1912, a Native American squad that challenged racist stereotypes. Historical photo via Wikipedia.

Community & Rituals

According to The Atlantic ("How Football Became America's Religion"), the sport functions like a modern form of worship, a weekly ritual that structures time, identity, and community. Stadiums, living rooms, and parking lots turn into sacred spaces where people gather to share emotion and tradition.

My Experience

My first football game in the United States changed my view of the sport completely. Before the game, we met at my friend's house to warm up and get ready. His dad drove us to the stadium, and from the moment we stepped out of the car, I could feel the energy.

Inside, everyone was friendly. People joined our photos, offered to take pictures for us, and talked to us even if we were strangers. When they heard it was my first-ever American football game, and that I wasn't from the U.S., they welcomed me immediately.

My first live football game under the stadium lights in Green Bay. Photo by Robert George (2025).

Even though the team played badly and lost, everyone still laughed, cheered, and enjoyed the night. I didn't feel like an outsider. I felt like part of the community.

"It felt like everyone was connected, even people who didn't know each other."

This experience helped me understand why football is such a strong sub-culture. It doesn't matter where you come from, once you're in the stadium, you belong.

Interview: College Player Perspective

Miami Dolphins wide receiver Tyreek Hill (10) runs with the ball against the Tennessee Titans at Hard Rock Stadium in Miami Gardens, Florida, on Sept. 30, 2024. Photo by Jasen Vinlove, via Fox News.

CP

Interview with a College-Age Player

1. What first got you interested in football, and what made you want to keep playing?

"I was first interested in football because of my older brothers, who were playing. Then I started to like it and always wanted to play in the NFL."

2. How old were you when you started playing, and who influenced you the most at that time?

"I was around 10 years old when I started playing tackle football, but I was always playing in the yard growing up. I was influenced most by my brothers and my dad."

3. Are there any football-related traditions you or your family have before or after games?

"When I was younger, we would always play catch in the yard during halftime of the Packers games. Now after my games, we always go out to eat."

4. In your opinion, why is football such a big deal in the U.S.?

"I think it is a great team sport that makes people want to watch it and be a part of it."

5. How does playing football make you feel part of a community or team?

"A football team is like its own tight-knit community. This builds good relationships; most of my close friends have all been on the football team."

6. What life lessons or values do you think football teaches players?

"It teaches you how to work with other people. You need to work hard to get to where you want to be. You have to be able to trust others that you work with."

7. There are many stereotypes about football players. Do you think any of them are true or unfair?

"One stereotype is that football players are dumb. I feel like this can be true, but just to be able to play the game you have to be fairly smart to know what’s going on."

8. How do you think the game has changed since you started playing?

"I think there has been a lot more emphasis on protecting the players, especially when it comes to head injuries."

9. What does ‘teamwork’ really mean to you on and off the field?

"It means that you have to trust your teammates that they are going to do what they're supposed to and you have to make sure to do your job."

10. Do you think football is becoming more popular or losing interest among young people? Why?

"I feel like there is less and fewer kids playing football recently. It might be because kids just don't like playing such a physical sport, and would rather play video games."

11. What role do families play in supporting players and keeping football traditions alive?

"They play a big role in supporting players, they are the ones that keep someone playing it. A supporting family will help motivate the player, but if they put too much pressure, it can push the player away from the game."

12. If you had to describe football culture in one word, what would it be and why?

"Brotherhood. You become very close to your teammates; it's like the team becomes another family."

Interview: Fan & Former Player Perspective

Green Bay Packers fans at Lambeau Field in a Spectrum News 1 story published on 7 September 2022 by Rachel Ryan.

MJ

Interview with a Lifelong Packers Fan & Former Player

1. What first got you interested in football?

"My dad was a football player in college, and his dad played in high school. I kind of just picked it up ever since I was young, and we were huge Packers fans. Watching the Packers and the Wisconsin Badgers was a big part of what got me into football."

2. How young were you when you started playing or watching?

"When I started playing, I was probably seven or eight years old in organized football. You start with flag football, and then in third grade I started playing tackle, which is kind of unheard of now because of concussions and restrictions."

3. Does football run in your family?

"Yeah. My dad played in college, I played in high school, and my grandpas on both sides also played high school football. That’s the connection, it’s been there for generations."

4. Why do you think football is so important in the United States?

"It was a sport that started in the U.S. and kind of embodies a lot about the country. The NFL started in 1920, and it’s blown up ever since. People want a team to cheer for. Here in Green Bay and in Wisconsin, everybody loves the Packers, and that ties people together."

5. How does being a fan make you feel part of a community?

"Being a fan means you share something in common with people. You can see someone on the street wearing Packers gear and start a conversation right away. When you see family, your uncle, your grandpa, you always end up talking about how the Packers are doing. It becomes a constant point of connection."

6. What values do you think football teaches?

"It teaches a lot about leadership and going against adversity. Those traits translate into your job and your family. You learn how to bounce back from tough situations. It also keeps you active and healthy during important years of your life, and that can change your long-term outcomes."

7. What stereotypes exist about football players or fans, and are they fair?

"A big stereotype is that football players are 'boneheads' or not smart. That’s not really true. There are guys in the NFL who got a 36 on their ACT or went to schools like Harvard. Ivy League schools have football teams full of really smart players. As for fans, some people stereotype them as thinking football is better than soccer, but it is more about culture and scale here."

8. How has the game changed over the years?

"At first the game was very rugby-style. Then the forward pass was introduced, and that revolutionized football. Quarterbacks and wide receivers became important, offenses got more explosive and fast-paced. Now it is also becoming more global, there is talk about the NFL expanding with a team outside the U.S., and more people around the world are paying attention."

9. What makes the Green Bay Packers unique in football culture?

"They’re a historic franchise with a lot of championships, but the big thing is that they’re fan-owned. Back in the 1920s they were having funding problems, so Curly Lambeau had people buy shares of the team. People still buy shares today. You don’t get many benefits except being able to say you’re an 'owner' and go to shareholders meetings where they talk about finances, future moves, and the state of the team. The Packers are known for being fan-owned, historic, and for having 13 NFL championships, the most ever."

10. Has football had a personal impact on your life?

"Yeah, football is tough. If you’re not good enough, you can’t always go to the next level. I chose to go into track and field instead, because I loved it more. There’s also a lot of risk with concussions, many people don’t want to keep playing because they want to protect their long-term health. I was lucky not to get any serious concussions."

For many people in Wisconsin, football is both a family tradition and a lifelong identity.

Cultural Analysis

American football meets all the criteria to be considered a sub-culture: it has its own spaces, rituals, values, and symbols. Whether in a small town, a college campus, or a massive NFL stadium, the same patterns of community and identity appear again and again.

Super Bowl flyover above a packed stadium from Andrew McGregor’s article "The Super Bowl: America’s Holiday" (US Sports History, 1 February 2016). Image courtesy of Wiki Commons.

Demographics

Players, fans, families, alumni, regions like Wisconsin where football is identity.

Rituals

Sundays, tailgating, jerseys, chants, family gatherings.

Values

Leadership, resilience, teamwork, national pride.

Beliefs

Football as identity, tradition, heritage.

Kinship & Tribe

Football matches the definition of a "tribe":

  • Unity
  • Shared identity
  • Collective values
  • Hierarchy (players, coaches, fans)
  • Ritualized gatherings

Outsider / Insider

Once you join, even as a foreigner, you are immediately welcomed.

Future of Football

Where is football going? The sport faces both expansion and risk. On one side, the NFL continues exploring games and fan bases outside the U.S. On the other, concerns about concussions and long-term injuries are changing how people think about playing the game.
