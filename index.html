History & Origins

American football has roots in post-Civil War culture, where young men needed a new arena to prove strength, discipline, and toughness. According to the Radiolab episode "American Football," early games at Harvard and Yale were brutal, chaotic, and often dangerous — yet celebrated as symbols of masculinity.

Early Ivy League Football

  • Eye gouging
  • Broken bones
  • No helmets
  • Violent scrimmages
"Football became the place where young men proved toughness when the Civil War was over." — Radiolab (2015)

Carlisle Indian School

Native American players used football as a tool to challenge racist stereotypes. Despite a system built to erase Native culture, Carlisle players earned national respect — redefining what Americans believed about identity and equality.

Community & Rituals

Weekly Rituals

Sunday games as sacred gatherings

Family Gatherings

Multi-generational participation

Tailgating

Pre-game celebrations and community building

Shared Symbols

Jerseys, chants, and team colors

Family-Based Tradition

Football is passed down like heritage:

  • Watching games together
  • Wearing team gear
  • Teaching kids the rules
  • Celebrating wins like holidays
"For many families, Sunday football has become the new church." — The Atlantic

My Experience

My first football game in the United States changed my view of the sport completely. Before the game, we met at my friend's house to warm up and get ready. His dad drove us to the stadium, and from the moment we stepped out of the car, I could feel the energy.

Inside, everyone was friendly. People joined our photos, offered to take pictures for us, and talked to us even if we were strangers. When they heard it was my first-ever American football game — and that I wasn't from the U.S. — they welcomed me immediately.

Even though the team played badly and lost, everyone still laughed, cheered, and enjoyed the night. I didn't feel like an outsider. I felt like part of the community.

"It felt like everyone was connected, even people who didn't know each other."

This experience helped me understand why football is such a strong sub-culture. It doesn't matter where you come from — once you're in the stadium, you belong.

Interview: Player Perspective

MJ

Interview with Former Football Player

1. How he got into football:

"My dad played in college and my grandpa played in high school, so I basically inherited football."

2. Starting young:

"I started playing organized football at around seven or eight. Third grade was when I started tackle — which today is rare because of concussion concerns."

3. Importance of football in the U.S.:

"It started here. People want a team to cheer for. Packers fans are some of the most die-hard fans in the country."

4. Community belonging:

"You can see someone wearing Packers gear on the street and start a conversation immediately."

5. Values learned:

Leadership

Overcoming adversity

Staying active

Responsibility

Teamwork

6. Stereotypes:

"A lot of people think players are dumb, but that's not true. There are NFL guys who scored a 36 on their ACT or went to Harvard."

7. Packers uniqueness:

"They're fan-owned. Fans literally buy shares and attend shareholder meetings."

8. Personal impact:

"I loved football, but I chose track and field. And concussions are a real concern."

Interview: Fan Perspective

SR

Interview with a Lifelong Football Fan

1. Growing up in a football family:

"I was born into it. My dad played, my grandpa played — football was always there."

2. Early memories:

"Watching Packers and Badgers games with my family since I was a kid."

3. Identity:

"In Wisconsin, football is part of who you are. It defines growing up here."

4. Belonging:

"When you're a fan, you instantly connect with others. It doesn't matter if you know them or not."

5. Traditions:

  • Game-days together
  • Jerseys
  • Asking each other about the team constantly
  • Talking about the Packers like they're family
"Here, football isn't a hobby — it's part of life."

Cultural Analysis

American football meets all the criteria to be considered a sub-culture:

Demographics

Players, fans, families, alumni, regions like Wisconsin where football is identity.

Rituals

Sundays, tailgating, jerseys, chants, family gatherings.

Values

Leadership, resilience, teamwork, national pride.

Beliefs

Football as identity, tradition, heritage.

Kinship & Tribe

Football matches the definition of a "tribe":

  • Unity
  • Shared identity
  • Collective values
  • Hierarchy (players, coaches, fans)
  • Ritualized gatherings

Outsider / Insider

Once you join (even as a foreigner), you're immediately welcomed.
